Information centre

Practical information for living in Preston

Tap an option below to read detailed guidance. This is not official advice, but a simple community guide to help Nepali members.

Finding jobs in Preston

  • Online job sites: Use UK job websites (Indeed, Reed, Totaljobs, LinkedIn). Search with keywords like Preston, warehouse, care assistant, kitchen staff, etc.
  • CV & cover letter: Prepare an English CV with UK phone number, address, work history and references. Keep it simple and clear.
  • Walk-in applications: Some restaurants, shops and warehouses accept CVs directly. Always be polite and ask if they are hiring.
  • Agencies: Recruitment agencies can help with warehouse, care and hospitality jobs. Members can share trusted agencies in the group.
  • Group help: In the WhatsApp group, members can share: job links, referrals, interview tips and warnings about bad employers.

Always check that offers are genuine. Never pay money just to apply for a job.

Medical system & GP registration (NHS)

  • Register with a GP: Find a local GP (doctor‚Äôs clinic) near your address. Every person should be registered with one GP practice.
  • Documents usually needed: ID (passport/BRP), proof of address (tenancy agreement, bill or bank letter). Some GPs accept new patients online.
  • Appointments: For non-emergency issues, call your GP or use their website to book a routine appointment or telephone consultation.
  • Urgent help: For urgent but not life-threatening issues, call NHS 111 or use 111 online. For emergencies (life in danger) call 999.
  • Prescriptions: After doctor consultation, you may receive a prescription to collect medicine from a pharmacy (charges may apply).

Members can share which GP surgeries are accepting new patients and their experience with local hospitals, dentists and pharmacies.

Opening a bank account

  • Choose a bank: Common options include high street banks (e.g. Barclays, NatWest, Santander) and online banks (e.g. Monzo, Starling).
  • Basic documents: Usually need ID (passport/BRP) and proof of address (tenancy agreement, bill, employer or university letter).
  • How to apply: Many banks allow account opening through their mobile app. Others require an appointment at a local branch.
  • Check fees: Look at card type, international transfer costs, and if the account is free or has monthly charges.
  • Safety: Never share your PIN or one-time codes in WhatsApp or with strangers. Bank staff will never ask for full passwords by phone.

Buying a SIM card & mobile plan

  • Main networks: EE, O2, Vodafone, Three and others. Supermarkets often sell SIMs (Tesco, ASDA, Aldi etc).
  • Pay-as-you-go vs contract: Pay-as-you-go is flexible with no long contract. Monthly contract gives more data but may require credit check.
  • What to check: Coverage in your area, data allowance, minutes for UK calls and international call options to Nepal.
  • Registration: Some SIMs require an app or website registration. Keep your UK address and ID ready.
  • Community help: Members can share which networks work best in different parts of Preston and which bundles are good value.

Other important information for new arrivals

  • National Insurance Number (NIN): For most jobs you need a National Insurance number. You can apply online on GOV.UK if you live in the UK, have the right to work and are working, looking for work or have a job offer.:contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}
  • Emergency numbers: 999 for emergency, 101 for non-emergency police, 111 for medical advice, 0800 numbers are usually free from mobiles.
  • Documents safety: Keep passport, BRP and contracts in a safe place. Take photos or scans for backup.
  • Scam awareness: Be careful with calls or messages asking for money or personal information. If unsure, ask the group before acting.
  • Language support: Many organisations offer translation help. Nepali friends in the group can also help explain things in simple language.

Student & immigration info

Student visa, PSW (Graduate), ILR & status

This section gives a simple overview for Nepali students and workers. It is based on official guidance as available in 2025, but rules can change. Always double-check on GOV.UK or with a qualified adviser before making any decision.

1) Student visa (for study in the UK)

  • You usually need an offer from a licensed sponsor (for example UCLan) and a Confirmation of Acceptance for Studies (CAS).:contentReference[oaicite:10]{index=10}
  • You must meet financial requirements and English language requirements set in the Immigration Rules.
  • Work during studies: On most full-time degree-level courses at a recognised university, students can usually work up to 20 hours per week in term-time and full-time in vacations, but your visa and university guidance will confirm your exact conditions.:contentReference[oaicite:11]{index=11}
  • You cannot claim most public funds/benefits and you cannot work as a professional sportsperson.:contentReference[oaicite:12]{index=12}
  • From 2024 onwards, most new students on taught courses cannot bring dependants unless on a postgraduate research programme (check GOV.UK for the latest change).

2) Graduate visa (Post-study work, ‚ÄúPSW‚Äù)

  • After successfully completing an eligible UK degree on a Student visa with a sponsor that has a track record of compliance, you may be able to apply for a Graduate visa.:contentReference[oaicite:13]{index=13}
  • As of late 2025, GOV.UK explains that the Graduate visa normally lets you stay for up to 2 years (3 years for PhD/doctoral graduates), during which you can work or look for work at most skill levels.:contentReference[oaicite:14]{index=14}
  • You must usually apply from inside the UK, after your university has told the Home Office that you have successfully completed your course.
  • You cannot extend the Graduate visa, but you might later switch to another visa, for example a Skilled Worker visa, if you meet the requirements.:contentReference[oaicite:15]{index=15}
  • The UK government has reviewed this route and may adjust the length or conditions in the future, so always read the latest official rules before applying.
üßæ Fee & IHS apply ‚Äì see GOV.UK for latest cost. ‚ö†Ô∏è Rules can change ‚Äì check before you apply.

3) Indefinite Leave to Remain (ILR ‚Äì settlement)

  • ILR is also called settlement. It lets you live, work and study in the UK without time limit and is normally needed if you want to apply for British citizenship.:contentReference[oaicite:16]{index=16}
  • Many work visa routes (for example Skilled Worker) give an option to apply for ILR after usually 5 years of continuous residence if you meet salary, absence and other conditions.:contentReference[oaicite:17]{index=17}
  • There is also a 10-year ‚Äúlong residence‚Äù route for people who have had continuous lawful residence in the UK for 10 years under various visas.:contentReference[oaicite:18]{index=18}
  • Most applicants aged 18‚Äì65 must pass the Life in the UK test and meet an English language requirement.
  • There have been discussions about changing qualifying periods in future, but you should rely on the current rules on GOV.UK when you apply.
üè° Goal: more secure long-term stay in the UK. üìö Life in the UK test & English required for most.

4) eVisa, share code & proving your status

  • The UK is moving to a digital system called an eVisa. Your status is held online instead of a physical vignette or BRP card.:contentReference[oaicite:19]{index=19}
  • You can view your eVisa and get a share code to prove your right to work, rent or travel using the official ‚ÄúView and prove your immigration status‚Äù service on GOV.UK.:contentReference[oaicite:20]{index=20}
  • Employers and landlords can check your status online using the share code and your date of birth. The code normally expires after 90 days.
  • Keep your email, phone number and passport details up to date in your UKVI account so that your eVisa matches your current document.
  • If you have any technical problems or cannot access your eVisa, use the helpline details given on GOV.UK or ask a trusted adviser for help.
üßæ Service: ‚ÄúView and prove your immigration status‚Äù. üì± Always use the official GOV.UK link.

5) National Insurance Number (NIN) & work

  • You normally need a National Insurance number if you live in the UK, have the right to work and are working, looking for work or have a job offer.:contentReference[oaicite:21]{index=21}
  • You apply online through GOV.UK and prove your identity (for example using a passport or national ID card).:contentReference[oaicite:22]{index=22}
  • You can usually start work before receiving your NIN if you can prove your right to work in the UK, but you should apply as soon as possible.:contentReference[oaicite:23]{index=23}
  • If you already have a NIN (for example printed on your BRP or from when you were younger) you do not need a new one ‚Äì it is the same for life.

FAQ

Frequently asked questions

Who can join the Nepalese in Preston (NIP) WhatsApp group?

Mainly Nepalese people living, working or studying in Preston and nearby areas. Friends of the Nepali community who want to support respectfully are also welcome.

Can I promote my business or YouTube/TikTok channel?

Only if it is clearly useful for the community and not spammy. Repeated promotion, unrelated links or clickbait are not allowed. Ask the admin first if unsure.

How do I report abuse or problems in the group?

Take a screenshot and contact the admin (Prabin Mahato) directly in private message. Do not start fights in the group; let the admin handle it.

Is the information on this website official advice?

No. This is a community-made guide based on shared experience. For legal, visa or medical issues always check official UK government or NHS websites.
